How to choose childcare in Perth: 7 questions every parent should ask

Updated June 2026 · A practical, honest guide for parents touring centres — including the questions most marketing brochures won't answer.

Choosing a childcare centre is one of the more emotionally loaded decisions you'll make as a Perth parent. The centres all have welcoming reception areas, smiling photos on Instagram, and a friendly tour guide. So how do you actually tell the difference between a centre that's right for your child and one that isn't?

The honest answer is: you ask better questions. The marketing language is broadly the same across the industry. What varies — significantly — is the day-to-day experience inside the rooms. This guide walks through the 7 questions we'd want any parent (including our own) to ask before signing an enrolment form.

1What is your educator-to-child ratio in this room?

Australian regulations set minimum ratios, and every approved centre has to meet them. The legal minimums for centre-based care are:

Age groupMinimum educator-to-child ratio
Birth to 24 months1 educator to 4 children
24 months to 36 months1 educator to 5 children
36 months and over (pre-school age)1 educator to 11 children

The number to ask about is the actual ratio in the room, today, not the regulatory minimum. Centres that consistently roster above minimum are choosing to spend more on educators — which is the single biggest predictor of the quality of your child's experience. Ask: "What's your typical staffing in this room?" then look around the room to see if it matches.

2What's your NQS rating, and when was your last assessment?

Every approved centre in Australia is rated against the National Quality Standard (NQS) by ACECQA — the regulator. Ratings are public on the ACECQA National Register.

There are five rating levels:

  • Excellent
  • Exceeding NQS
  • Meeting NQS
  • Working Towards NQS
  • Significant Improvement Required

"Meeting NQS" is a solid result — it means the centre satisfies all seven quality areas. "Exceeding" means the centre goes above the standard in multiple areas. If a centre is "Working Towards", that's not automatically a deal-breaker (many newly opened centres get this), but ask what specific quality areas they're working on, and what their improvement plan looks like.

Also worth asking: when was the last assessment? Ratings can be several years old, so the picture today may be different from the rating on the register.

3What does a typical day look like — and how flexible is it?

This question separates "structured" centres from "rigid" ones, and "play-based" from "unfocused". You want to hear specifics: meal times, sleep times, the rough balance of indoor and outdoor play, and how the day is shaped around the children's interests.

Ask follow-ups like: "What does morning look like for a 2-year-old who's slow to settle?" or "How do you handle a 4-year-old who really wants to keep drawing when it's time to pack up?" The answers tell you whether educators are trained to follow the child's emotional state, or whether they're focused mainly on running the schedule.

What to listen for: A confident answer will include specific examples ("Last week one of our toddlers was struggling with drop-off, so we…") rather than generic principles ("We respect each child's individual needs"). Specifics beat slogans every time.

4How do you handle settling-in for new children?

The first 4-6 weeks are usually the hardest for the child and the parent. A thoughtful settling-in process makes an enormous difference. Look for centres that:

  • Offer one or two free visits before the official start date so your child can meet their educators
  • Have a clear plan for separation anxiety — and tell you what they'll do if your child is crying at drop-off in week 2
  • Communicate proactively in the first month (a quick text or photo at lunchtime, not just when there's a problem)
  • Welcome a comfort item from home — a soft toy, a small blanket — without restrictions

A red flag here is vague reassurance ("Oh, they'll be fine within a week"). The good centres take settling seriously because they know it shapes your child's whole experience.

5How do you communicate with parents?

Most Perth centres now use a parent app (OWNA, Xplor, Kinderloop are the common ones) to share daily observations, photos, meal records and sleep times. Ask which one the centre uses and whether you can see a sample of yesterday's posts during your tour.

Beyond the app, ask:

  • Who do I talk to if I have a concern — the educator, the room leader, or the centre director?
  • How quickly do you respond to parent messages outside the app?
  • Do you do formal parent-educator conversations? How often?
  • How are incidents (a bump, a bite, a missed nap) communicated?

You want a culture where small concerns are easy to raise. The centres that get this right will have a clear name and contact point for you, not a vague "speak to anyone."

6What does the food look like — and who cooks it?

Food sounds like a small detail, but it tells you a lot about the centre. Cooking on-site (vs heating frozen meals) usually signals a centre that invests in the daily experience. Ask:

  • Are meals cooked on-site, or pre-prepared off-site?
  • Can I see this week's menu?
  • How do you accommodate allergies and cultural preferences?
  • What happens at meal-times — do educators sit and eat with the children?

If you can, ask to taste a sample (most centres are happy to oblige on a tour). And check the menu for variety — a four-week rotation with fresh vegetables in each meal is a fair benchmark.

7What are the all-in fees, and how does CCS apply for my family?

This is the question parents save for the end of the tour, which usually backfires — you walk out without a clear picture and only the gut sense that "it'll be a few hundred a week." Ask for:

  • The daily session fee (LDC) or per-session fee (OSHC)
  • What's included — meals, sunscreen, nappies, excursions
  • What's not included and might be charged extra
  • An estimate of your weekly out-of-pocket after Child Care Subsidy (CCS) — most centres can run a quick calc if you give them your family income and activity hours

If you'd like the CCS basics first, our plain-English CCS guide walks through eligibility, the percentage scale, and how to apply.

Tip for the tour: Take a notebook (or your phone's notes app). Centres start to blend together after the second or third tour — and the small specific details that swayed you in the moment are exactly what you'll forget by Friday.

Red flags to notice on a tour

Beyond the questions above, trust what you see. Be cautious if:

  • The educators look stretched — multiple children crying, no one circulating, the tour guide is also the only adult with the babies
  • The director can't tell you the centre's current NQS rating off the top of their head
  • Your specific questions get generic answers ("We follow the EYLF" — every centre does)
  • The tour skips the rooms during nap time (sometimes appropriate, sometimes a way to hide a thin staffing roster)
  • You're pressured to sign an enrolment form on the spot

Common parent questions

Q: How many centres should I tour before deciding?

A: Three is usually enough. Two so you have something to compare; three so you can see a pattern. More than four and the details start to blur.

Q: When should I start looking?

A: For LDC (long day care), 3-6 months before you need a place. Popular Perth centres run waitlists, especially for under-2 rooms. For OSHC, 2-3 months ahead of Term 1 is usually safe — Vacation Care fills faster.

Q: My child has additional needs — how do I find the right centre?

A: Be upfront on the tour. Ask about the Inclusion Support Programme (ISP), any educators with additional training, and how the centre has supported children with similar needs in the past. A good centre will engage thoughtfully; a poor fit will downplay or deflect.

Q: How do I compare centres if they're all "Exceeding NQS"?

A: Look at which quality areas they're exceeding in. A centre exceeding in Quality Area 5 (Relationships with children) tells you something different from one exceeding in Quality Area 3 (Physical environment). Ratings are public — read the assessment report, not just the headline.

Q: Is a brand-new centre worth considering?

A: It can be, but go in with eyes open. New centres often have shiny facilities and motivated teams — but no track record. Ask who the director is, where their previous centres were rated, and how settled the educator team is. Turnover in year one matters.
